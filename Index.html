Case study

Traffic-based profitability model for OOH/DOOH advertising in Casablanca

Traffic simulation, spatial mapping and financial modelling for advertising-location investment appraisal.

Back to Transition 5.0 projectsDownload CV

Executive Summary

This project evaluated how road traffic, socio-economic context, regulatory constraints and cost structure influence the financial attractiveness of outdoor advertising locations in Casablanca.

Problem

Advertising locations are often selected using visibility intuition. This project tested whether traffic simulation, GIS mapping and financial modelling could produce a more disciplined investment-ranking logic.

Data / Inputs

  • SUMO traffic simulation outputs
  • GIS / spatial mapping
  • Socio-economic and demographic indicators
  • Regulatory constraints
  • CAPEX
  • OPEX
  • TCO
  • ROI assumptions

Methodology

  1. Model traffic flows with SUMO.
  2. Map zones and corridors with GIS.
  3. Add socio-economic and regulatory criteria.
  4. Build CAPEX/OPEX/TCO scenarios.
  5. Calculate ROI and break-even logic.
  6. Construct IRP ranking.

Tools

SUMO, QGIS/GIS mapping, Excel financial modelling, CAPEX/OPEX/TCO scenario logic, ROI and break-even analysis.

Outputs

Potential Profitability Index, five-year OOH/DOOH investment scenarios, location-attractiveness logic, traffic-exposure maps and simulation dashboard.

Results

Scenario-based estimate: The strongest scenario produced a projected maximum ROI of 533% and a 5-week break-even point under the stated assumptions.

Limitations

The outputs are scenario-based and depend on simulated traffic, estimated cost assumptions, occupancy assumptions and regulatory conditions. Real deployment would require field validation.

What this proves about me

This proves I can connect mobility data, spatial analytics and finance into an investment-ranking model, while keeping assumptions visible.

Case study

AI-MCDM investment scorecard for smart-city infrastructure

A structured scoring framework for infrastructure investment under carbon, energy and geoeconomic uncertainty.

Back to Transition 5.0 projectsDownload CV
Executive SummaryProblemData / InputsMethodologyToolsOutputsResultsLimitationsWhat this provesEvidence links

Executive Summary

This research developed a decision-support framework for ranking smart-city infrastructure projects under carbon, energy and geoeconomic uncertainty.

Problem

Traditional NPV-only infrastructure evaluation can ignore carbon-price exposure, energy volatility, geopolitical risk and non-financial public-value criteria.

Data / Inputs

  • World Bank WDI / ESG indicators
  • World Bank PPI database
  • EIB project and pipeline records
  • EIBIS indicators
  • Carbon-price scenarios
  • GPR index
  • Constructed 25-project scoring matrix

Methodology

  1. XGBoost demand forecasting
  2. Ridge benchmark comparison
  3. Carbon-adjusted DNPV
  4. Real Options valuation
  5. Fuzzy-AHP / MEREC weighting
  6. AROMAN ranking
  7. Sensitivity analysis
  8. Cross-method comparison

Tools

Python workflow, XGBoost, Ridge regression, DNPV, Real Options, Fuzzy-AHP, MEREC, AROMAN, TOPSIS/COPRAS comparison, sensitivity analysis.

Outputs

25-project ranking framework, validation outputs, sensitivity tests, auditability/traceability disclosure and replication-package logic.

Results

  • Model-validation result: 45.8% lower MAE versus Ridge in original temporal validation.
  • Self-developed model: 25-project ranking framework.
  • Exploratory research output: Carbon-price threshold effect.
  • Rankings diverged from NPV-only ranking.
  • Framework supports auditability, traceability and sensitivity disclosure.

Limitations

The scorecard is a decision-support framework, not an objective optimization engine. Some tests remained exploratory, and H3 was not confirmed at full panel level.

What this proves about me

This proves I can build structured AI-assisted investment appraisal models and communicate uncertainty instead of hiding it.

Case study

Cost-carbon control model for sustainable procurement

A procurement-control architecture linking Scope 3, ISO 14083, OPEX/CAPEX and supplier evaluation.

Back to Transition 5.0 projectsDownload CV
Executive SummaryProblemData / InputsMethodologyToolsOutputsResultsLimitationsWhat this provesEvidence links

Executive Summary

This proposal designs a procurement control architecture that helps buyers compare supplier cost, carbon intensity, data reliability and fleet-transition risk in one evaluative routine.

Problem

Scope 3 logistics emissions may be measured, but they often remain outside the procurement routines that shape supplier selection, contract monitoring and investment appraisal. Emissions visibility alone does not tell a buyer how to score a carrier, monitor a contract, or justify a cleaner but more expensive logistics option.

Data / Inputs

  • Scope 3 logistics emissions data
  • ISO 14083 transport-chain emissions logic
  • GLEC framework
  • OPEX, CAPEX and TCO variables
  • Supplier performance indicators
  • Fleet-transition risk assumptions

Methodology

  1. Define cost-carbon procurement problem.
  2. Integrate ISO 14083 / GLEC logic.
  3. Map OPEX, CAPEX and TCO variables.
  4. Build supplier-control indicators.
  5. Design Sustainability Balanced Scorecard prototype.
  6. Evaluate through Design Science Research.

Tools

Design Science Research, Sustainability Balanced Scorecard, ISO 14083, GLEC, Scope 3 accounting logic, OPEX/CAPEX/TCO modelling.

Outputs

Cost-carbon control architecture, supplier evaluation routine, data-quality escalation logic, contract review indicators and procurement-facing dashboard concept.

Results

Self-developed model: The scorecard is framed as procurement decision control, not decorative ESG reporting.

Limitations

The proposal requires verified carrier data or a minimum viable dataset before it can be empirically tested in a real procurement setting.

What this proves about me

This proves I can translate sustainability measurement into managerial control routines for supplier selection, contract monitoring and investment appraisal.

Executive Summary

This experience track shows applied analytics execution in real business settings: financial-performance analysis, reporting automation, dashboards, portfolio segmentation and investment modelling.

Problem

Organizations often have useful operational and financial data, but decision-makers need cleaner reporting, segmentation, dashboards and scenario logic to act on it.

Data / Inputs

  • NPL portfolio data
  • Client portfolio and credit-risk information
  • Advertising-location financial assumptions
  • Recovery and collection KPIs
  • Business-unit reporting workflows

Methodology

  1. Segment portfolios by risk and aging profile.
  2. Build Excel dashboards and reporting workflows.
  3. Automate repeated reporting steps with VBA and Power Query.
  4. Model ROI, CAPEX, OPEX and TCO scenarios.
  5. Standardize outputs for management reporting.

Tools

Advanced Excel, VBA, Power Query, Power BI, financial modelling, KPI dashboards, portfolio analysis.

Outputs

Dashboard outputs, reporting automation, portfolio segmentation, scenario models and standardized management reporting.

Results

  • Professional workflow result: 40% reporting-cycle reduction through VBA and Power Query automation.
  • 97.9B MAD non-performing-loan portfolio evaluated through segmentation logic.
  • 20+ corporate client portfolios analyzed for financial performance and credit-risk exposure.

Limitations

Internship outputs are business-context specific and depend on internal data quality, reporting requirements and management definitions.
